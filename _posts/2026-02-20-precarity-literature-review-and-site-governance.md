## 1-page summary

This meeting combined two priorities: strengthening the analytical basis of Nepero's public narrative and finalizing concrete website and governance choices. Marco opened with a personal update that his long-awaited two-year research contract had finally started, after a period of unemployment. This framed the discussion in practical terms: progress on the initiative depends on members working under unstable and changing labor conditions.

The first half focused on literature review work about precarity in research. The group discussed how broad the field is and how strongly it intersects with economic, sociological, and political analyses of neoliberal labor systems. A key point was that research precarity is not a marginal subtopic but a structural mechanism of the broader precarious labor regime. The conversation highlighted a specific vulnerability of researchers: many remain in unstable conditions because they are highly motivated by the work itself, which can be exploited institutionally through competition and career uncertainty.

They also noted a practical contradiction in scholarly communication: important review papers on precarity are sometimes inaccessible behind paywalls, including to public research institutions outside those disciplinary subscription bundles. This reinforced the group's broader critique of publishing infrastructures and strengthened the case for open and community-controlled dissemination.

On outputs, participants converged on a layered strategy. The deeper investigation should become a rigorous written draft (potentially publishable), while a shorter and clearer synthesis should be adapted for the website. In parallel, they proposed preparing a reusable 15-20 minute presentation format that can be used in local events, political spaces, and cross-community discussions.

The second half of the meeting addressed implementation details for the website. They discussed adding a public noticeboard-style section where ideas and collaboration proposals can be posted with low entry barriers, then evaluated naming and interaction norms for that section. Additional decisions included improving the join flow (including automation for signups), clarifying who is publicly responsible for the initiative, and ensuring there is enough transparency about the people behind the project.

Finally, they discussed shared expenses for infrastructure already purchased for the site, preferring a simple collective fund model rather than ad-hoc reimbursements. The meeting closed with task assignments: continue the solutions-focused literature work, draft the synthesis section for publication, and advance website features needed for public onboarding.
