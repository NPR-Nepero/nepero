## Summary

This meeting combined political discussion, conceptual development, and practical implementation. The starting point was a shared frustration with the way research funding is organized around highly individualized leadership models: one person accumulates resources, defines the agenda, and then recruits precarious researchers into subordinate roles. From there, the group asked a sharper question than in earlier meetings: what would an actual collaborative alternative look like if it were designed from the perspective of precarious researchers themselves?

The first major idea discussed was a collaborative mode of research that does not simply reproduce existing hierarchies at a smaller scale. Participants explored the possibility of projects built by dispersed precarious researchers who share an interest in a problem and contribute complementary skills, rather than working under a single principal investigator. They noted that current funding schemes rarely allow this kind of horizontal collaboration, because precarious researchers are usually not treated as autonomous subjects capable of initiating collective research on their own terms.

This led directly to the idea of a journal or journal section centered on questions rather than only results. Giampaolo proposed a format in which researchers publish pieces that articulate important open questions, break them into sub-questions, and use publication itself as a way to attract collaborators. Francesco and Marco helped clarify that the point is not to delegate work to others, but to create a mechanism for meeting people with complementary expertise. In this discussion, the Wall emerged as a simplified first implementation of that broader vision: a place where someone can post a short abstract of a project they want to pursue and indicate that they are looking for collaborators.

The meeting then moved from concept to implementation. The group discussed how the Wall should function at the current stage, treating it essentially as a structured post on the site, with the possibility of expanding later if the experiment proves useful. This was connected to a broader conversation about the future journal: if the Wall works, it could eventually become a regular section or even inform a larger publication model dedicated to collaborative and question-driven research.

The second half of the meeting focused on technical infrastructure. Mauro explained the current static-site workflow based on Jekyll and Markdown, while Giampaolo described the dynamic layer hosted on Altervista for interactive elements such as sign-up or login-related features. Francesco joined this part of the discussion to understand how the static GitHub version and the dynamic server version coexist, how files are deployed, and how future interactive features could be developed without breaking the simpler publishing workflow.

The meeting closed with outreach planning. The group discussed preparing a short slide deck or concise presentation that introduces both the broader issue of academic precarity and NePeRo's specific initiative. They agreed that the website should now serve as a concrete base for contacting people again, and that a first outreach email should link to the site and frame it as the first tangible output of the group. Practical tasks were assigned around finishing a few missing website elements, drafting the outreach email, and using the improved site to bring new people into the conversation.
