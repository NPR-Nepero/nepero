## 1-page summary

This meeting opened in a tense political and emotional atmosphere, with discussion of war, bombardment, and the concrete effects that large-scale violence can have on the lives and mental stability of researchers and their families. As in earlier sessions, this was not treated as a separate issue from academic precarity, but as part of the wider instability within which scientific work and collective organizing now take place.

The main working block was dedicated to the resources section on precarity. Marco reported progress on the literature review, especially on how academic precarity fits within the broader field of precarity studies. A key thread was that researchers are a particularly revealing case because many remain in unstable conditions not only through institutional pressure, but also through strong attachment to research itself. This makes academic precarity both structurally produced and ideologically reinforced, and helps explain why it has generated a large and internally self-reflective literature.

The group then shifted from diagnosis to evidence and solutions. Marco highlighted policy-oriented material, including a substantial Austrian report on precarity in European academia, while Mauro worked on the historical and quantitative side by collecting data on contract structures in the United States and Italy. Their discussion emphasized that the growth of precarious academic labor is not accidental, but tied to long-term institutional choices: expansion of research systems through temporary labor, stagnation in permanent positions, and the multiplication of contract types that obscure who is structurally precarious and who is not.

This analytical work fed directly into website construction. Mauro edited the resources page in real time, while the group discussed how to define precarity carefully, how much institutional language from sources such as the European Parliament should be incorporated, and how to balance conceptual clarity with practical readability. They also agreed that the site should present not only critique, but enough comparative material across countries to make the problem legible beyond a single national case.

Another major topic was the new "Wall" section. The idea is to create a moderated space for short research proposals or project abstracts that do not fit normal career incentives but could attract collaborators through shared curiosity. The group discussed likely constraints immediately: moderation would be necessary, the format should remain short and legible, and entries should probably be handled as discrete files in a way similar to posts. The underlying aim was to give visibility to intellectually serious ideas that are difficult to pursue inside ordinary professional networks.

The final part of the meeting turned to public outreach. Mauro described parallel organizing work around a manifesto that had already collected many signatures and expressions of interest from potential active members. This led to discussion of how NePeRo itself should present its work to broader audiences: through a first structured online event, through a reusable slide deck of roughly fifteen to twenty minutes, and through more explicit presentation of who the organizers are and what the initiative is concretely trying to build. The meeting closed with a practical division of labor: continue refining the literature-and-data section, prepare initial wall content, broaden the country examples, and use the improved website as a basis for wider circulation.
