## Summary

This meeting took place in a reduced format, with Giampaolo and Mauro using the time for a more open discussion that moved between immediate career pressures, the internal logic of academic precarity, and broader questions about the direction of contemporary research. It was a less operational meeting than some of the previous ones, but still strongly connected to NePeRo's core themes: how institutional constraints shape what kinds of science can be done, and under what conditions researchers are able to think freely.

The first part of the conversation focused on applications, mobility, and the exhaustion produced by repeated transitions between positions. Both participants reflected on the amount of time lost every year to relocation, job hunting, and adaptation to new groups. They described this not only as a personal burden but as a structural distortion of research itself: short contracts and serial mobility interrupt continuity, force people to adjust to external agendas, and make long-term intellectual development much harder.

This led into a deeper discussion about publication strategy and survival. Giampaolo explicitly raised the problem of research becoming tied to livelihood, arguing that when publication directly determines whether one can continue working, the content of research is inevitably shaped by that dependence. The conversation contrasted scientific curiosity with the strategic logic of maximizing article counts, splitting projects into smaller publishable units, and aligning work with what hiring or funding systems currently reward.

Another important thread was the pressure to relabel or redirect research toward fashionable areas such as artificial intelligence, even when the underlying work is more general or theoretical. This opened into a long scientific exchange between the two participants about machine learning, statistical physics, generalization, benchmarks, latent structure, and the interpretation of model behavior. Rather than being a digression, this part of the meeting made concrete one of the collective's recurring concerns: researchers are pushed to package work in ways that match current institutional demand, even when the most interesting questions remain more abstract and foundational.

The meeting also briefly touched on NePeRo's website, with Mauro reporting small improvements to readability and acknowledging that neither participant had made much progress on the collective project that week. That admission itself was part of the substance of the meeting: the initiative continues to depend on time carved out from unstable and demanding work situations.

Overall, this session functioned as a reflective checkpoint. It did not produce major website or organizational decisions, but it sharpened the group's diagnosis of how precarious labor, publication metrics, and topical fashions interact to narrow intellectual freedom. It also reaffirmed that one purpose of NePeRo is to defend spaces where deeper, slower, and less strategically distorted research conversations can still happen.
